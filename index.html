STOP 1

Motor Court - The Arrival

LOOK AROUND YOU
Find the Eros statue and imagine a car pulling into the court with invited guests.

This was an arrival space, but not the mansion's grand 'front' in the way the Lunar Lawn facade was. Even here, the experience was carefully composed. Hillwood describes the Eros statue as a welcoming figure facing the drive. Marjorie bought Hillwood in 1955 and transformed the property into both a highly functioning residence and a setting for collections she already understood might one day be seen by the public.

Think of Hillwood as a production: guests saw elegance, but many people worked behind the scenes to make that elegance appear effortless.

STOP 2

Lunar Lawn - Hillwood as a Social Stage

LOOK AROUND YOU
Face the mansion from the lawn. Notice how the house becomes a backdrop.

The Lunar Lawn is an outdoor room shaped for gathering. Hillwood's archival material shows Marjorie hosting a garden tea here in 1969. Picture tables, dresses, conversation, staff moving discreetly, and the mansion framing the scene.

Entertaining was one of the ways Marjorie built relationships, supported causes, and participated in Washington social life. A successful gathering required planning: flowers, food, seating, guest lists, staff, weather contingencies, and atmosphere.

STOP 3

Japanese-style Garden - A Collector Designs a World

LOOK AROUND YOU
Find a stone lantern, bridge, symbolic figure, or a contrast between Asian and North American plants.

Landscape designer Shogo Myaida created this garden as a blend rather than a strict reconstruction of a traditional Japanese garden. Hillwood notes that Myaida believed a garden should suit both the property and the personality of the client. One archival photograph shows Marjorie guiding students through this garden in 1963.

That image is a clue to how she saw Hillwood: it was private, but she also enjoyed showing and explaining what she had assembled.

STOP 4

French Parterre - Taste, Identity, and Image

LOOK AROUND YOU
Notice the symmetry, clipped plantings, paths, walls, fountain, and sense of order.

The French Parterre evokes an eighteenth-century European formal garden. That choice connects directly to Marjorie's interest in French decorative arts and the visual language of elegance, control, and ceremony.

A designed environment can express taste, but it can also communicate identity. Hillwood lets us ask how much a home says who someone is - and how much it says who they want others to see.

STOP 5

Rose Garden - A Life Remembered

LOOK AROUND YOU
Find the central monument and notice the garden's circular form.

Landscape architect Perry Wheeler adapted this garden for Marjorie in 1956. Today it is also her memorial. More than a year after her 1973 death, her ashes were placed in the base of the central monument. The inscription includes her motto, translated by Hillwood as 'All my hopes rest in me.'

Marjorie inherited enormous wealth, but legacy is not the same as inheritance. Hillwood reflects what she chose to collect, support, preserve, and ultimately share.

STOP 6

Friendship Walk & Four Seasons - The Surprise Gift

LOOK AROUND YOU
Find the Friendship Walk plaque, then continue toward the circular Four Seasons Overlook.

For Marjorie's seventieth birthday in 1957, friends secretly organized a tribute with help from her head gardener and landscape architect Perry Wheeler. Hillwood says 181 friends contributed. Marjorie was led to the completed walk and a plaque honoring her generosity, love of beauty, and concern for human needs.

She later showed the path to visitors but also used it for solitary reflection. Friendship here is both social network and private memory.

STOP 7

Putting Green - Recreation at a Grand Estate

LOOK AROUND YOU
Count how many holes you can see. Observe from the edge rather than walking on the turf.

The nine-hole putting green was not just decoration. Hillwood says Marjorie sometimes brought out golf balls on cool evenings for putting competitions with family and friends. The estate once included other recreational facilities as well.

Hillwood was formal, but it was not frozen. People played, exercised, talked, competed, and relaxed here.

STOP 8

Dacha - Russia, Memory, and the Cold War

LOOK AROUND YOU
Notice the log construction, carved details, bright colors, and onion-shaped roof forms.

The Dacha was built in 1969, during the Cold War, but its inspiration reaches back to Marjorie's time in Moscow in the 1930s. Hillwood describes it as a nostalgic interpretation rather than a perfectly authentic Russian dacha.

The timing is fascinating: while the United States and Soviet Union were geopolitical rivals, Marjorie created a building celebrating a version of Russian cultural heritage.

STOP 9

Greenhouse & Cutting Garden - The Work Behind Beauty

LOOK AROUND YOU
Look for orchids, tropical plants, or flowers being grown for display and arrangements.

Hillwood's floral displays required planning and labor. Marjorie employed an orchid curator, and blooming orchids were placed throughout the mansion. The Cutting Garden supplied flowers used in formal arrangements typical of the period.

Historic photographs help today's staff recreate the style and placement of those arrangements. The estate's elegance depended on many people whose work visitors did not always see.

STOP 10

Dog Cemetery - The Personal Marjorie

LOOK AROUND YOU
Read a few of the names and dates on the memorials.

Marjorie loved dogs throughout her life. Hillwood records that her last dog, Scampi, was buried here in 1972, and members of her family continued memorializing pets here into the 1980s.

Historical estates can make people seem larger than life. A place like this restores something ordinary and recognizable: attachment, loss, memory, and affection.
